To show that a parallelogram is a rectangle, we need to see that the enclosed angles are \(90^\circ\text{.}\) If we think of two adjacent sides of the parallelogram as vectors \(\mbf{x}\) and \(\mbf{y}\text{,}\) then its other two sides are translates of \(\mbf{x}\) and \(\mbf{y}\text{,}\) as depicted in Figure 2.2.29:

We now see that the two diagonals can be described by the vectors \(\mbf{x}+\mbf{y}\) and \(\mbf{x} - \mbf{y}\text{:}\)

If we assume that our parallelogram's diagonals have the same length, then by the above argument, that means that \(\norm{\mbf{x}+\mbf{y}} = \norm{\mbf{x}-\mbf{y}}\text{.}\) We can therefore apply Exercise 2.2.5.12 to the vectors \(\mbf{u}:= \mbf{x}+\mbf{y}\) and \(\mbf{v}:= \mbf{x} - \mbf{y}\text{:}\) Since \(\norm{\mbf{u}} = \norm{\mbf{v}}\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\mbf{u} + \mbf{v}\) and \(\mbf{u} - \mbf{v}\) are orthogonal. Plugging in the definition of \(\mbf{u}\) and \(\mbf{v}\text{,}\) this means that
\begin{equation*} (\mbf{x}+\mbf{y}) + (\mbf{x} - \mbf{y}) = 2\mbf{x} \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} (\mbf{x}+\mbf{y}) - (\mbf{x} - \mbf{y}) = 2\mbf{y} \end{equation*}
are orthogonal, and therefore, so are \(\mbf{x}\) and \(\mbf{y}\text{.}\) Since twp adjacent sides of the parallelogram are orthogonal, it is a rectangle.
