1. We want the dot product of the two vectors to be zero, i.e. we want

    \begin{equation*} 0 \stackrel{!}{=} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = 2x + (-1)(-2) + 3\cdot 1 = 2x + 5 \end{equation*}

    Thus, we need \(x=\frac{-5}{2}\text{.}\)

  2. To use the Cosine FormulaÂ 1.2.5.1, we have to compute the norm of the vectors and their dot product:

    \begin{align*} \norm{\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} } \amp = \sqrt{\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \cdot\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} } = \sqrt{ 2^2 + (-1)^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{6}\\ \norm{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ x \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} } \amp = \sqrt{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ x \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \cdot\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ x \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} } = \sqrt{ 1^2 + x^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{5+x^2}\\ \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ x \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \amp = 2\cdot 1 + (-1)\cdot x + 1\cdot 2 = 4-x \end{align*}

    Therefore,

    \begin{equation*} \cos (\theta) \stackrel{!}{=} \frac{ 4-x }{\sqrt{6}\sqrt{5+x^2}}, \text{ i.e. } \cos (\theta)\sqrt{6}\sqrt{5+x^2} \stackrel{!}{=} 4-x . \end{equation*}

    Since \(\cos (\frac{\pi}{3}) = \frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) we can rewrite that to

    \begin{equation} \sqrt{6(5+x^2)} \stackrel{!}{=} 2(4-x) = 8-2x .\tag{1.2.1} \end{equation}

    If we square both sides of this equation, we get

    \begin{align*} \amp 6(5+x^2) = 30 + 6x^2 \stackrel{!}{=} (8-2x)^2 = 64 - 32x + 4x^2\\ \iff\amp -34 + 32x + 2x^2 \stackrel{!}{=} 0\\ \iff\amp -17 + 16x + x^2 \stackrel{!}{=} 0\\ \iff\amp x \stackrel{!}{=} -\frac{16}{2} \pm \sqrt{ \left(\frac{16}{2}\right)^2 - (-17) } = -8 \pm \sqrt{ 81 } = -8 \pm 9\\ \iff\amp x \stackrel{!}{=} 1 \text{ or } x \stackrel{!}{=} -17 \end{align*}

    Therefore, the answer is: for \(x=1\) and \(x=-17\text{,}\) the angle between the two vectors is \(\frac{\pi}{3}\text{.}\)
