Section 1.2 Length and Angle: The Dot Product

¶

Exercises Exercises

1.

(Source: [4], Section 4.2, Exercise 4.2.1.)

Compute \(\mathbf{u \cdot v}\) where:
  1. \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3\end{bmatrix} \) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \)
  2. \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -1\end{bmatrix} \) and \(\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}\text{.}\)
  3. \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ -3\end{bmatrix} \) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \)
  4. \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ 5\end{bmatrix} \) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ -7 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix} \)
  5. \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z\end{bmatrix} \) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{bmatrix} \)
  6. \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z\end{bmatrix} \) and \(\mathbf{v} = 0\)
Hint
The dot product of two vectors is the sum of the product of the components.
Answer
  1. \(0\)
  2. \(6\)
  3. \(-2\)
  4. \(0\)
  5. \(x\cdot a + y\cdot b + z\cdot c\)
  6. \(0\)
Solution
Recall: the dot product of two vectors is the sum of the product of the components.
  1. \begin{align*} \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = 2\cdot (-1) + (-1)\cdot 1 + 3\cdot 1 = -2 -1+3 = 0. \end{align*}
  2. \begin{align*} \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -1\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -1\end{bmatrix} = 1\cdot 1 + 2\cdot 2 + (-1)\cdot (-1) = 1+4+1 = 6. \end{align*}
  3. \begin{align*} \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ -3\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = 1\cdot 2 + 1\cdot (-1) + (-3)\cdot 1 = 2-1-3=-2 \end{align*}
  4. \begin{align*} \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ 5\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ -7 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix} = 3\cdot 6 + (-1)\cdot (-7)+5\cdot(-5)\\ \amp = 18 + 7 -25 = 0. \end{align*}
  5. \begin{align*} \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{bmatrix} = x\cdot a + y\cdot b + z\cdot c. \end{align*}
  6. \begin{align*} \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = x\cdot 0 + y\cdot 0 + z\cdot 0 = 0 + 0 + 0 = 0. \end{align*}

2.

(Source: [4], Section 4.1, Exercise 4.1.1.)

Compute \(\norm{\mathbf{v}}\) if \(\mathbf{v}\) equals:
  1. \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \)
  2. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \)
  3. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1\end{bmatrix} \)
  4. \(\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \)
  5. \(2\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \)
  6. \(-3\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \)
Hint
Definition: The norm\(\norm{\mathbf{v}}\) of a vector \(\mbf{v}\) is defined as
\begin{equation*} \norm{\mathbf{v}} = \sqrt{\mbf{v}\cdot \mbf{v}} . \end{equation*}
Answer
  1. \(3\)
  2. \(\sqrt{6}\)
  3. \(\sqrt{2}\)
  4. \(\sqrt{5}\)
  5. \(2\sqrt{6}\)
  6. \(\sqrt{54}\)
Solution
  1. \begin{align*} \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = 2\cdot 2 + (-1)\cdot (-1)+ 2\cdot 2 = 4 +1+4=9 . \end{align*}

    Therefore, \(\norm{\mbf{v}}=\sqrt{\mbf{v}\cdot\mbf{v}} = 3\text{.}\)

  2. \begin{align*} \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = 1\cdot 1+(-1)\cdot (-1)+2\cdot 2 = 1+1+4 = 6 . \end{align*}

    Therefore, \(\norm{\mbf{v}}=\sqrt{\mbf{v}\cdot\mbf{v}} =\sqrt{6}\text{.}\)

  3. \begin{align*} \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1\end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = 1\cdot 1+0\cdot 0+(-1)\cdot (-1) = 1+0+1 =2 . \end{align*}

    Therefore, \(\norm{\mbf{v}}=\sqrt{\mbf{v}\cdot\mbf{v}} =\sqrt{2}\text{.}\)

  4. \begin{align*} \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 \amp = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = (-1)\cdot (-1) + 0\cdot 0 + 2\cdot 2 = 1+0+4 = 5 . \end{align*}

    Therefore, \(\norm{\mbf{v}}=\sqrt{\mbf{v}\cdot\mbf{v}} =\sqrt{5}\text{.}\)

  5. \begin{align*} \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 \amp = \left( 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \right) \cdot \left( 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \right)\\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -2 \\ 4\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -2 \\ 4\end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = 2\cdot 2 + (-2)\cdot (-2) + 4\cdot 4 = 4+4+16 = 24 . \end{align*}

    Therefore, \(\norm{\mbf{v}}=\sqrt{\mbf{v}\cdot\mbf{v}} =\sqrt{4\cdot 6}=2\sqrt{6}\text{.}\)

  6. \begin{align*} \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 \amp = \left(-3\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \right) \cdot \left(-3\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \right)\\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ -3 \\ -6\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ -3 \\ -6\end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = (-3)\cdot (-3) + (-3)\cdot (-3) + (-6)\cdot (-6) = 9+9+36 = 54 \end{align*}

    Therefore, \(\norm{\mbf{v}}=\sqrt{\mbf{v}\cdot\mbf{v}} =\sqrt{54}\text{.}\)

3.

(Source: [4], Section 4.1, Exercise 4.1.2.)

Find a unit vector in the direction of:
  1. \(\begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ -1 \\ 5\end{bmatrix} \)
  2. \(\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \)
Hint 1
Definition: A vector \(\mbf{v}\) is called unit vector if \(\norm{\mbf{v}}=1.\)
Hint 2
Given a non-zero vector \(\mbf{w}\text{,}\) what norm and direction does \(\mbf{v}=\frac{1}{\norm{\mbf{w}}}\mbf{w}\) have?
Answer
  1. \(\begin{bmatrix} \frac{7}{5\sqrt{3}} \\ \frac{-1}{5\sqrt{3}} \\ \frac{5}{5\sqrt{3}}\end{bmatrix} \)
  2. \(\begin{bmatrix} \frac{-2}{3} \\ \frac{-1}{3} \\ \frac{2}{3}\end{bmatrix} \)
Solution

  1. We first compute the square of the norm of the vector:

    \begin{align*} \norm{\begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ -1 \\ 5\end{bmatrix} }^2 \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ -1 \\ 5\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ -1 \\ 5\end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = 7^2 + (-1)^2 + 5^2 = 49+1+25 = 75, \end{align*}

    so that \(\norm{\begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ -1 \\ 5\end{bmatrix} } = \sqrt{75}=5\sqrt{3}\text{.}\) Thus, a unit vector in the same direction is given by

    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\norm{\begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ -1 \\ 5\end{bmatrix} }} \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ -1 \\ 5\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{7}{5\sqrt{3}} \\ \frac{-1}{5\sqrt{3}} \\ \frac{5}{5\sqrt{3}}\end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

  2. We first compute the square of the norm of the vector:

    \begin{align*} \norm{\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} }^2 \amp = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = (-2)^2+(-1)^2+2^2 = 4+1+4 = 9 , \end{align*}

    so that \(\norm{\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} } = \sqrt{9}=3\text{.}\) Thus, a unit vector in the same direction is given by

    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\norm{\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} }} \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{-2}{3} \\ \frac{-1}{3} \\ \frac{2}{3}\end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

4.

(Source: [4], Section 4.1, Exercise 4.1.4.)

Find the distance between the following pairs of points:
  1. \(( 3 , -1 , 0)\) and \(( 2 , -1 , 1 )\)
  2. \(( 2 , -1 , 2)\) and \(( 2 , 0 , 1)\text{.}\)
  3. \(( -3 , 5 , 2)\) and \(( 1 , 3 , 3 )\)
  4. \(( 4 , 0 , -2)\) and \(( 3 , 2 , 0 )\)
Hint
The distance from a point \(P\) to a point \(Q\) is the length of the vector from \(P\) to \(Q\text{.}\)
Answer
  1. \(\sqrt{2}.\)
  2. \(\sqrt{2}.\)
  3. \(\sqrt{21}.\)
  4. \(3.\)
Solution
We need to find the length of the vector between the points.
  1. For \(P=( 3 , -1 , 0)\) and \(Q=( 2 , -1 , 1 )\text{,}\) we compute:
    \begin{equation*} \vect{PQ} = \begin{bmatrix} 2-3 \\ -1-(-1) \\ 1-0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\vect{PQ}} = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + 0^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}. \end{equation*}
  2. For \(P=( 2 , -1 , 2)\) and \(Q=( 2 , 0 , 1)\text{,}\) we compute:
    \begin{equation*} \vect{PQ} = \begin{bmatrix} 2-2 \\ 0-(-1) \\ 1-2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\vect{PQ}} = \sqrt{0^2 + 1^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{2}. \end{equation*}
  3. For \(P=( -3 , 5 , 2)\) and \(Q=( 1 , 3 , 3 )\text{,}\) we compute:
    \begin{equation*} \vect{PQ} = \begin{bmatrix} 1-(-3) \\ 3-5 \\ 3-2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\vect{PQ}} = \sqrt{4^2 + 2^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{21}. \end{equation*}
  4. For \(P=( 4 , 0 , -2)\) and \(Q=( 3 , 2 , 0 )\text{,}\) we compute:
    \begin{equation*} \vect{PQ} = \begin{bmatrix} 3-4 \\ 2-0 \\ 0-(-2) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\vect{PQ}} = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + 2^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{9}=3. \end{equation*}
We should point out that, in each of the above cases, we could just as well have computed \(\norm{\vect{QP}}\) and would have gotten the same result.

5.

(Source: [2], Section 2.6, Exercise 2.6.4.)

Find \(\cos(\theta)\) where \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \;\text{ and }\; \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
Hint
Cosine formula: The angle between two non-zero vectors \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) is the number \(\theta \in [0,\pi)\) which satisfies
\begin{equation*} \cos (\theta) = \frac{ \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v}}{ \norm{\mbf{u}}\cdot \norm{\mbf{v}}} . \end{equation*}
Notice that \(\cdot\) in the expression \(\norm{\mbf{u}}\cdot \norm{\mbf{v}}\) is just multiplication of real numbers, while \(\cdot\) in the expression \(\mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v}\) is the dot product of vectors.
Answer
\begin{equation*} \cos (\theta) = \frac{-3}{ \sqrt{231}} \end{equation*}
Solution

According to the Cosine Formula 1.2.5.1, we have to compute the norm of the vectors and their dot product:

\begin{align*} \norm{\mbf{u}} \amp = \sqrt{\mbf{u}\cdot\mbf{u}} = \sqrt{ 3^2 + (-1)^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{11}\\ \norm{\mbf{v}} \amp = \sqrt{\mbf{v}\cdot\mbf{v}} = \sqrt{ 1^2 + 4^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{21}\\ \mbf{u}\cdot\mbf{v} \amp = 3\cdot 1 + (-1)\cdot 4 + (-1)\cdot 2 = -3 \end{align*}

Therefore,

\begin{equation*} \cos (\theta) = \frac{ \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v}}{ \norm{\mbf{u}}\cdot \norm{\mbf{v}}} = \frac{-3}{ \sqrt{11}\cdot \sqrt{21}} = \frac{-3}{ \sqrt{231}} . \end{equation*}

6.

(Source: [1], Section 4.7, Exercise 4.7.9.)

Find the angle between the vectors
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \;\text{ and }\; \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -7 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
Hint
Look back at the Cosine Formula 1.2.5.1.
Answer
\begin{equation*} \theta =\mathrm{arcos} \left(\frac{-5}{9}\right) \end{equation*}
Solution

According to the Cosine Formula 1.2.5.1, we have to compute the norm of the vectors and their dot product:

\begin{align*} \norm{\mbf{u}} \amp = \sqrt{\mbf{u}\cdot\mbf{u}} = \sqrt{ 1^2 + (-2)^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{6}\\ \norm{\mbf{v}} \amp = \sqrt{\mbf{v}\cdot\mbf{v}} = \sqrt{ 1^2 + 2^2 + (-7)^2} = \sqrt{54}\\ \mbf{u}\cdot\mbf{v} \amp = 1\cdot 1 + (-2)\cdot 2 + 1\cdot (-7) = -10 \end{align*}

Therefore,

\begin{equation*} \cos (\theta) = \frac{ \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v}}{ \norm{\mbf{u}}\cdot \norm{\mbf{v}}} = \frac{-10}{ \sqrt{6}\cdot \sqrt{54}} = \frac{-10}{18} = \frac{-5}{9} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \theta =\mathrm{arcos} \left(\frac{-5}{9}\right) . \end{equation*}

7.

(Source: [4], Section 4.2, Exercise 4.2.3.)

Find all real numbers \(x\) such that:
  1. \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \) and \(\begin{bmatrix} x \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \) are orthogonal.
  2. \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \) and \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ x \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \) are at an angle of \(\frac{\pi}{3}\text{.}\)
Hint 1
Two vectors \(\mbf{u}\) and \(\mbf{v}\) are called orthogonal if their dot product is zero, i.e. \(\mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{v} = 0\text{.}\)
Hint 2
Look back at the Cosine Formula 1.2.5.1.
Answer
  1. \(x=\frac{-5}{2}\)
  2. \(x=1\) and \(x=-17\)
Solution

  1. We want the dot product of the two vectors to be zero, i.e. we want

    \begin{equation*} 0 \stackrel{!}{=} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = 2x + (-1)(-2) + 3\cdot 1 = 2x + 5 \end{equation*}

    Thus, we need \(x=\frac{-5}{2}\text{.}\)

  2. To use the Cosine Formula 1.2.5.1, we have to compute the norm of the vectors and their dot product:

    \begin{align*} \norm{\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} } \amp = \sqrt{\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \cdot\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} } = \sqrt{ 2^2 + (-1)^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{6}\\ \norm{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ x \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} } \amp = \sqrt{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ x \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \cdot\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ x \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} } = \sqrt{ 1^2 + x^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{5+x^2}\\ \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ x \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \amp = 2\cdot 1 + (-1)\cdot x + 1\cdot 2 = 4-x \end{align*}

    Therefore,

    \begin{equation*} \cos (\theta) \stackrel{!}{=} \frac{ 4-x }{\sqrt{6}\sqrt{5+x^2}}, \text{ i.e. } \cos (\theta)\sqrt{6}\sqrt{5+x^2} \stackrel{!}{=} 4-x . \end{equation*}

    Since \(\cos (\frac{\pi}{3}) = \frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) we can rewrite that to

    \begin{equation} \sqrt{6(5+x^2)} \stackrel{!}{=} 2(4-x) = 8-2x .\label{ex-dot-product-solve-for-angle-eq}\tag{1.2.1} \end{equation}

    If we square both sides of this equation, we get

    \begin{align*} \amp 6(5+x^2) = 30 + 6x^2 \stackrel{!}{=} (8-2x)^2 = 64 - 32x + 4x^2\\ \iff\amp -34 + 32x + 2x^2 \stackrel{!}{=} 0\\ \iff\amp -17 + 16x + x^2 \stackrel{!}{=} 0\\ \iff\amp x \stackrel{!}{=} -\frac{16}{2} \pm \sqrt{ \left(\frac{16}{2}\right)^2 - (-17) } = -8 \pm \sqrt{ 81 } = -8 \pm 9\\ \iff\amp x \stackrel{!}{=} 1 \text{ or } x \stackrel{!}{=} -17 \end{align*}

    Therefore, the answer is: for \(x=1\) and \(x=-17\text{,}\) the angle between the two vectors is \(\frac{\pi}{3}\text{.}\)

8.

(Source: [1], Section 4.7, Exercise 4.7.10.)

Find the \(\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{w})\) where
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{w} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} , \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
Hint
Definition: If \(\mbf{v}\) and \(\mbf{w}\) are two vectors in \(\mbb{R}^n\) and \(\mbf{v}\neq \mbf{0}\text{,}\) then the projection of \(\mbf{w}\) onto \(\mbf{v}\) is the vector \(\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{w})\) defined by
\begin{equation*} \operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{w}) = \left( \frac{\mbf{v}\cdot \mbf{w}}{\mbf{v}\cdot \mbf{v}} \right) \mbf{v} . \end{equation*}
Answer
\(\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{-5}{14} \\ \frac{-10}{14} \\ \frac{-15}{14} \end{bmatrix} \)
Solution
Since
\begin{equation*} \operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{w}) = \left( \frac{\mbf{v}\cdot \mbf{w}}{\mbf{v}\cdot \mbf{v}} \right) \mbf{v} , \end{equation*}
we should first compute the dot products:
\begin{align*} \mbf{v}\cdot \mbf{w} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} = 1\cdot 1 + 2\cdot 0 + 3\cdot (-2) = -5,\\ \mbf{v}\cdot \mbf{v} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = 1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 = 14, \text{ and so }\\ \amp \frac{\mbf{v}\cdot \mbf{w}}{\mbf{v}\cdot \mbf{v}} = \frac{-5}{14} . \end{align*}
Therefore,
\begin{equation*} \operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{w}) = \frac{-5}{14} \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{-5}{14} \\ \frac{-10}{14} \\ \frac{-15}{14} \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

9.

(Source: [2], Section 2.6, Exercise 2.6.14.)

Decompose the vector \(\mathbf{v}\) into \(\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}\) where \(\mathbf{a}\) is parallel to \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) is perpendicular to \(\mathbf{u}\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2\\ -5 \end{bmatrix} , \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
Hint 1
Write down the definition of \(``\mathbf{a}\) is parallel to \(\mathbf{u}"\) and use the condition \(``\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}"\) to find a formula for \(\mathbf{b}\) as linear combination of \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{u}\text{.}\)
Hint 2
Write down the definition of \(``\mathbf{b}\) is perpendicular to \(\mathbf{u}"\text{,}\) and then replace \(\mbf{b}\) with your solution to Hint 1.2.9.1.
Answer
\(\mbf{a} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{-27}{38} \\ \frac{-18}{38}\\ \frac{45}{38}\end{bmatrix} \text{,}\) \(\mathbf{b}= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{65}{38} \\ \frac{-20}{38}\\ \frac{31}{38}\end{bmatrix} \)
Solution
We require \(\mbf{a}\) to be parallel to \(\mbf{u}\text{,}\) i.e. \(\mbf{a} = c \mbf{u}\) for some real number \(c\) that is yet to be determined. Plugging that into \(\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}\text{,}\) we get a definition of \(\mbf{b}\) that also depends on \(c\text{:}\) \(\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{v} - c \mbf{u}\text{.}\) As \(\mbf{b}\) needs to be orthogonal to \(\mbf{u}\text{,}\) we want \(\mbf{b}\cdot \mbf{u}=0\text{.}\) If we plug our tentative solution for \(\mbf{b}\) into this, we get
\begin{equation*} (\mathbf{v} - c \mbf{u})\cdot \mbf{u}=0 . \end{equation*}
Using the properties of the dot product, we arrive at
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{v}\cdot \mbf{u} = c \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{u}, \text{ i.e. } c = \frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot \mbf{u}}{\mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{u}} \text{ using that }\mbf{u}\neq \mbf{0} . \end{equation*}
We can now compute both sides and get a value for \(c\text{:}\)
\begin{align*} \mathbf{v}\cdot \mbf{u} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2\\ -5 \end{bmatrix} = 1\cdot 3 + (-1)\cdot 2 + 2\cdot (-5) = -9,\\ \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{u} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2\\ -5 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2\\ -5 \end{bmatrix} = 3^2 + 2^2 + (-5)^2 = 38, \text{ so that } \end{align*}
\begin{equation*} c = \frac{-9}{38}. \end{equation*}
We can now solve for our vectors \(\mbf{a}\) and \(\mbf{b}\text{:}\)
\begin{gather*} \mbf{a} = c \mbf{u} = \frac{-9}{38}\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2\\ -5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{-27}{38} \\ \frac{-18}{38}\\ \frac{45}{38}\end{bmatrix} \\ \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{v} - \mbf{a} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} \frac{-27}{38} \\ \frac{-18}{38}\\ \frac{45}{38}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{65}{38} \\ \frac{-20}{38}\\ \frac{31}{38}\end{bmatrix} . \end{gather*}
Let us do a sanity check: Is our \(\mbf{b}\) indeed orthogonal to \(\mbf{u}\text{?}\) We compute:
\begin{align*} \mbf{b}\cdot \mbf{u} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{65}{38} \\ \frac{-20}{38}\\ \frac{31}{38}\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2\\ -5 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{65}{38} \cdot 3 + \frac{-20}{38}\cdot 2 + \frac{31}{38}\cdot (-5)\\ \amp = \frac{195-40-155}{38} = 0, \end{align*}
so yes!

12.

(Source: [2], for Chapter II, Section "Length and Angle Measures", Problem 24.)

Show that \(\norm{\mathbf{u}} = \norm{\mathbf{v}}\) if and only if \(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}\) are perpendicular. Give an example in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\)
Hint 1
Remember that an if and only if statement goes both ways.
Hint 2
Use the properties of the dot product to find a formula for the dot product of \(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}\) in terms of \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\text{.}\)
Solution

Since we are making a claim about the dot product of \(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}\text{,}\) let us use the properties of the dot product to simplify it:

\begin{align} (\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v})\cdot(\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}) \amp = (\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v})\cdot\mathbf{u} - (\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v})\cdot\mathbf{v} \amp\amp (\text{Distributivity})\notag\\ \amp = (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}) - (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}) \amp\amp (\text{Distributivity})\notag\\ \amp = \norm{\mbf{u}}^2 + \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} - \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 \amp\amp (\text{definition of } \norm{\cdot})\notag\\ \amp = \norm{\mbf{u}}^2 + \mathbf{u}\cdot \mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} - \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 \amp\amp (\text{Commutativity})\notag\\ \amp = \norm{\mbf{u}}^2 - \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 .\label{ex-same-norm-iff-two-things-are-perp-manipulation}\tag{1.2.2} \end{align}

The two vectors being perpendicular means exactly that their dot product is zero, i.e.

\begin{equation*} (\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v})\perp (\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}) \iff (\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v})\cdot(\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}) = 0 \end{equation*}

By (1.2.2), this happens if and only if \(\norm{\mbf{u}}^2 - \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 = 0\text{,}\) or in other words:

\begin{equation*} (\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v})\perp (\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}) \iff \norm{\mbf{u}}^2 = \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 \iff \norm{\mbf{u}}= \norm{\mbf{v}} , \end{equation*}

as claimed.

For example, the vectors \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \) have the same norm since

\begin{equation*} \norm{\mbf{u}}^2 = \mbf{u}\cdot \mbf{u} = 3^2 + 4^2 = 25 \;\text{ and }\; \norm{\mbf{v}}^2 = \mbf{v}\cdot \mbf{v} = 5^2 + 0^2 = 25 . \end{equation*}

We check that indeed

\begin{align*} (\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v})\cdot (\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}) \amp = \left( \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \right) \cdot \left( \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \right)\\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 3+5 \\ 4+0 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 3-5 \\ 4-0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = 8\cdot (-2) + 4\cdot 4 = 0. \end{align*}

If the diagonals of a parallelogram have equal length, show that the parallelogram is a rectangle.

Solution
To show that a parallelogram is a rectangle, we need to see that the enclosed angles are \(90^\circ\text{.}\) If we think of two adjacent sides of the parallelogram as vectors \(\mbf{x}\) and \(\mbf{y}\text{,}\) then its other two sides are translates of \(\mbf{x}\) and \(\mbf{y}\text{,}\) as depicted in Figure 1.2.2:

We now see that the two diagonals can be described by the vectors \(\mbf{x}+\mbf{y}\) and \(\mbf{x} - \mbf{y}\text{:}\)

If we assume that our parallelogram's diagonals have the same length, then by the above argument, that means that \(\norm{\mbf{x}+\mbf{y}} = \norm{\mbf{x}-\mbf{y}}\text{.}\) We can therefore apply Exercise 1.2.12 to the vectors \(\mbf{u}:= \mbf{x}+\mbf{y}\) and \(\mbf{v}:= \mbf{x} - \mbf{y}\text{:}\) Since \(\norm{\mbf{u}} = \norm{\mbf{v}}\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\mbf{u} + \mbf{v}\) and \(\mbf{u} - \mbf{v}\) are orthogonal. Plugging in the definition of \(\mbf{u}\) and \(\mbf{v}\text{,}\) this means that
\begin{equation*} (\mbf{x}+\mbf{y}) + (\mbf{x} - \mbf{y}) = 2\mbf{x} \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} (\mbf{x}+\mbf{y}) - (\mbf{x} - \mbf{y}) = 2\mbf{y} \end{equation*}
are orthogonal, and therefore, so are \(\mbf{x}\) and \(\mbf{y}\text{.}\) Since twp adjacent sides of the parallelogram are orthogonal, it is a rectangle.
